Abstract  9 
The biocatalytic recycling of plastics, such as polyethylene terephthalate (PET), promises a sustainable 10 
alternative to our present open-loop cycles. Engineering of PET-hydrolases for this purpose has focused on 11 
improving activity near the glass-transition temperature of the polymer by increasing their thermostability, 12 
neglecting other features of the protein-polymer system that affect enzymatic activity. Here, we isolate the effect 13 
of electrostatics on the activity of a thermophilic PETase by rationally redesigning its surface charge, while 14 
preserving its thermodynamic properties. The enzyme variant, SfInv, shows orders of magnitude improvements 15 
in binding affinity and in activity towards untreated plastic films, with inverted morphological preference. When 16 
combined, the wildtype enzyme and SfInv act synergistically, revealing an entirely new mechanism for 17 
cooperative activities driven by complimentary electrostatic interactions at the PET surface. These findings 18 
highlight unexplored avenues in improving PETase function through the control of morphological preference or 19 
introduction of protein cooperativity by exploiting protein electrostatics.  20 
Introduction  21 
Using enzymes for biocatalytic depolymerisation of plastics provides a low-energy and low-resource alternative to 22 
the current open-loop recycling of common consumer materials1. In the case of polyethylene terephthalate 23 
(PET), this process allows for the nearly quantitative recovery of the constituent monomers terephthalic acid 24 
(TPA) and ethylene glycol (EG), making new routes to (bio)-chemical valorisation and repolymerisation 25 
accessible2,3. The range of PETases, enzymes capable of carrying out this conversion, has also been expanded in 26 
recent years through discovery and further engineering4â€“8. Most of these research efforts have targeted large-27 
scale bio-recycling through increases in enzymatic thermostability, allowing for improved reaction rates and 28 
depolymerisation extents by taking advantage of higher polymer chain mobility near the bulk PET glass transition 29 
temperature (Tg)9â€“14. This primary focus on thermostability has deepened our understanding of 30 
thermodynamically important structural features of PET-degrading enzymes;10,14 however, it has left obscure 31 
much of the fundamental nature of the interactions between the proteins and the polymer surface, which 32 
ultimately governs enzymatic selectivity, optimal conditions and industrial performance. 33 
Plastic-degrading enzymes face a multitude of challenges associated with the physical-chemical properties of 34 
their solid, hydrophobic substrate beyond polymer chain dynamics, which cannot be overcome by enhanced 35 
enzymatic thermostability and higher reaction temperatures. On solid substrates, catalytic activities can be 36 
influenced by restricted diffusion, variations in the density of surface charge, degree of crystallinity, and protein-37 
protein interactions15â€“19. These complications have been shown to underlie some of the more counter intuitive 38 
behaviours we observe in natural PETases, such as diminished activities at elevated enzyme 39 
concentrations,17,20,21 which prevent some members of the enzyme family from being used at an industrial scale. 40 
Studying such attributes can therefore clarify fundamental enzyme-polymer interactions underlying catalytic 41 
activities, allowing us to better engineer biocatalysts towards industrial recycling applications. 42 
In this study we examine SfCut, a highly active, thermostable PETase from Saccharopolyspora flava most closely 43 
related to PHL78 (Figure 1), previously reported4 to have an exceptional preference for micronized PET powder 44 
over amorphous PET film. We sought to understand the molecular basis for this morphological preference, 45 
hoping to gain insight into how substrate specificity is controlled. Through focused structural analysis, we 46 
identified that the principal feature differentiating SfCut from other PETases was its markedly negative charge; to 47 
explore the implications of this property, we used a rational protein design approach that maintained the 48 
thermodynamic stability of SfCut while inverting its overall charge. This allowed us to clarify the role of protein 49 
electrostatics in substrate morphological preference and provided new insights into how we can use protein 50

Results and Discussion  54 
Structural analysis of SfCut and surface charge redesign  55 
To understand the parameters that influence the substrate morphological preference of SfCut, we carefully 56 
examined its structure. Like other reported PETases, SfCut (PDB: 7QJP) adopts a typical alpha-beta hydrolase 57 
fold, with a conserved disulfide bond between Cys243 and Cys260. The active site matches that of a typical PET-58 
degrading serine hydrolase, composed of a catalytic triad (Ser132, Asp178 and His210), a conserved tryptophan 59 
(Trp157), an oxyanion hole (Phe64 and Met133) and a lipase box. Despite these features, SfCut is strikingly 60 
different to other highly active PETases owing to its negative surface charge and total charge density of -0.55 61 
per kDa at neutral pH. These charges are mostly found on the surface opposite to the active site, away from 62 
residues expected to directly interact with the plastic (Figure 2a/c). This pronounced charge appears to modulate 63 
the morphological selectivity of SfCut, causing its activity to drop considerably between pH 6.0 and pH 7.5 on 64 
films while it increases on amorphous powders4. As PET has a stable, negative zeta potential independent of pH 65 
at these conditions22, the changes in depolymerisation rates implicate the involvement of both protein and 66 
polymer electrostatics as significant factors in these properties. 67 
We aimed to investigate how protein charge influences substrate morphology preference of SfCut by extensively 68 
redesigning its surface. To ensure clarity in our analysis, we sought to isolate the effects of charge substitutions 69 
from inadvertent changes to enzyme activity caused by variations in protein thermostability. To avoid this, we 70 
selected a manual design approach over computational methods, as the later have been constructed in a way 71 
that produces stabilised proteins as outputs. For design purposes, all charged residues in the crystallographic 72 
structure, including histidines involved in salt bridges, were identified and counted towards the overall protein 73 
charge (-16). Owing to the lack of observed crystallographic density, we did not count the first and last glutamic 74 
acid residues in the sequence. 75 
To minimise disruption of the protein-polymer interface and the active site, only residues at a distance greater 76 
than 10 Ã… from the catalytic triad were considered for mutation. For the purposes of design, we ranked residues 77 
based on how amenable to change they might be, excluding those that were buried, involved in critical polar 78 
contacts, stabilising secondary structural elements, or providing an ambiguous structural role. Our choices of 79 
sites for mutation were supported by referencing the position-specific scoring matrix (PSSM) of SfCut 80 
(Supplementary Spreadsheet 1) excluding residues at highly conserved sites. As a priority for mutation, we 81 
focused on changing isolated negatively charged residues on Sf Cutâ€™s surface, considering structural context to 82 
minimise steric clashes and preserve native contacts. Once all the free residues amenable to change were 83 
exhausted, we proceeded to consider mutations within larger salt-bridge clusters followed by neutral surface 84 
residues. In the interest of investigating solely the effect of the surface charge, we did not intentionally introduce 85 
mutations that could create new stabilising interactions.  86 
The redesigned variant, here referred to as Sf Inverse (SfInv), incorporates 24 mutations (Supplementary Table 87 
2; Supplementary Figure 2) at sites with an average information content of 0.41, resulting in an overall charge of 88 
+16 (Î”charge of +32) and a charge density of + 0.54 per kDa at neutral pH. Structural predictions using 89 
ColabFold23,24 and ESMfold25 (pLDDT > 0.9, Supplementary Figure 3) closely align with the crystal structure of 90 
SfCut, having CÎ± RMSD values of 0.38 Ã… and 0.52 Ã…, respectively. These results suggested that the redesign was 91 
likely to preserve the protein fold, without introducing structural changes that could impact enzymatic function.  92

The role of surface charge on protein-polymer interactions 124 
To explore how their significant difference in protein surface charge manifested in terms of depolymerase activity 125 
across PET morphologies, we examined SfCut and SfInv on three PET substrates: amorphous powder (aPow), 126 
amorphous film (aFilm) and semi-crystalline powder (cPow) (Figure 3a, Supplementary Figure 7). SfInv 127 
demonstrated higher activity than the wildtype enzyme across all substrates tested, with broader pH and 128 
temperature optima, as well as three-fold and six-fold more product release on semi-crystalline powders and 129 
amorphous powders respectively. Our results confirmed previous observations that SfCut has a considerably 130 
lower activity on films compared to powders, independent of substrate crystallinity. In contrast, SfInv showed an 131 
inverted selectivity: its activity on films was enhanced, approximately 2-fold higher than on powders, 132 
demonstrating that enzyme specificity for different substrate morphologies is a genetically encoded, engineerable 133 
property. This corresponds to over 200-fold enhancement in the activity on amorphous PET films compared to 134 
SfCut, effected simply by surface charge inversion. Despite the substantial difference in evident binding affinity 135 
to amorphous powders, both enzymes show activity saturation on amorphous films above 250 nM, confirming 136 
that binding alone is insufficient to explain their catalytic properties (Supplementary Figure 8). Although not an 137 
intended outcome of our design process, SfInv demonstrated a similar level of conversion as the most promising 138 
industrialised enzyme, LCCICCG, 10, at their respective optima (Figure 3a). In a direct comparison at 50 Â°C, SfInv 139 
outperformed LCCICCG at laboratory scale (Supplementary Figure 9). 140 
Although the greatly enhanced binding affinity observed with SfInv can partially explain its higher enzymatic 141 
activity, differences in enzymatic morphological preference must be caused by a change in how the differently 142 
charged enzymes interact with the polymer. Electrostatic interactions between the polymer surface and the 143 
enzymes can be mediated by both unspecific and specific interactions with ions in solution, therefore we 144 
assessed the activities of both enzymes at a range of ionic strengths, using both monovalent and divalent salts. 145 
The negatively charged wildtype enzyme, SfCut, showed a monotonic rise in activity on amorphous films as ionic 146 
strength increased from 0 M to 4 M, with a relative activity enhancement of nearly 600-fold (Figure 3c, & 147 
Supplementary Figure 10a). This behaviour contrasts with that of some of the best-performing PETases, such as 148 
LCCICCG, where the depolymerase activity is not affected by the ionic strength of the solution (Figure 3c & 149 
Supplementary Figure 10b). For SfInv the effect of ionic strength was less pronounced (Figure 3c), with activity 150 
peaking at 250 mM ionic strength at approximately five-fold higher than in buffer with 0 M salt, before declining 151 
to approximately 7% of its maximal value at 2 M (Supplementary Figure 10a). These results on amorphous films 152 
were consistent across different salts (Supplementary Figure 10), indicating that the selectivity observed in SfCut 153 
and SfInv is driven by electrostatic interactions between the enzyme and the polymer surface, rather than by 154 
specific interactions mediated by salts. 155 
Interestingly, the effect of ionic strength on the enzyme activity was distinct on amorphous powders. SfInv 156 
showed the same monotonic rise in activity as SfCut, although less pronounced (Supplementary Figure 10c), with 157 
both enzymes seeing no benefit to activity above an ionic strength of 1 M. Overall, positively charged SfInv sees 158 
relatively moderate benefits of increased ionic strength, with a greater effect observed on powders than films, 159

SfInv shows greatly enhanced PET-degrading activity at pilot scale and on post-consumer waste 170 
The improved selectivity of SfInv towards amorphous PET films under analytical conditions suggested a potential 171 
for digesting unmodified amorphous films at pilot scale, pH-controlled experiments. Under these conditions (1 172 
mgenzyme gPET-1 with high solids loading of 20% w/v)1,29, the wildtype enzyme (SfCut) demonstrates limited 173 
activity, achieving less than 1% substrate conversion and a monomeric product yield of 0.15 g/L as measured by 174 
HPLC. In contrast, the engineered SfInv exhibited significantly higher efficiency, achieving approximately 8% 175 
substrate conversion within 24 hours (Figure 4a), with a monomer yield of 18.6 g/L. Strikingly, at substantially 176 
lower enzyme loading (1 ï­M, ~0.15 mgenzyme gPET-1), SfInv achieved the same high levels of conversion within the 177 
same time frame, suggesting there is substantial potential for reaction condition optimisation to maximise the 178 
digestion of amorphous PET films while reducing resource requirements (Supplementary Figure 12).  179 
Comparable results were also found to be true on untreated post-consumer waste (PCW) in the form of PET-film 180 
sandwich trays (Figure 4b, Supplementary Table 5, Supplementary Figures 13 and 14). SfInv efficiently digested 181 
over 10% of the waste within 24 hours, achieving a measured yield of 19.25 g/L, while SfCut yielded negligible 182 
amounts of product (Figure 4b). This further highlights that it may be possible to engineer enzymes specifically 183 
targeting the processing of complex post-consumer waste streams of PET with limited pre-treatments, upstream 184 
of current methods reliant on resource-intensive preparation of micronized powders.29 185

for the enzymes to work synergistically in degrading PET. Specifically, we hypothesised that the amount of 190 
product released from a mixture of the two enzymes could exceed the sum of the product release for each 191 
enzyme individually at its respective concentration. To quantify this, we define synergy as:  192

To further clarify the mechanism of the synergistic activity, we explored potential causes involving the differing 201 
enzyme charges. One possibility was that the enzymes might assemble in solution in a way that enhances their 202 
stability and activity. To test this, we incubated the enzymes together at elevated concentrations (~ 34 ï­M, 203 
compared to the 0.05-1 ï­M enzyme concentrations used in polymer digestions) and evaluated whether they form 204 
complexes by size exclusion chromatography on the mixture (Figure 5e); no shift was observed in the elution 205 
profile, suggesting that the enzymes do not appreciably bind to each other in solution. To exclude the possibility 206 
that the enzymes are targeting different sites on the polymer chain and therefore modifying the plastic surface 207 
such that it becomes a better substrate for the oppositely charged enzyme, we examined whether the enzymes 208 
showed a stepwise synergy. The activities of the enzymes were evaluated on amorphous PET film coupons that 209 
had been pre-treated with either SfCut or SfInv (Figure 5d), with no significant difference in activity observed on 210 
films pre-treated with either enzyme, showing that the surface targets for both enzymes are largely the same.  211

experiments. In this case, SfInv binds to the plastic surface masking the negative charges of the polymer and 214

observed with the use of synthetic surfactants30, where anionic detergents were used to increase the negative 216 
surface charge of the polymer, therefore attracting the cationic IsPETase31 to the surface, accelerating enzymatic 217

The difference in how the film surface is modified by the enzymes acting individually or as a mixture can be 219 
visualised after partial digestion of PET coupons using SEM (Figure 5b). SfCut increases the roughness of the 220 
surface leaving what appears to be thread-like crystalline regions behind, whilst SfInv shows broad pitting, a 221 
surface modification commonly observed with other highly active PETases18,31. However, with the enzyme 222

As far as we are aware, this is the first time that synergy has been observed in enzymes directly modifying 226 
plastic surfaces. These results reveal a previously unexplored mechanism by which enzyme mixtures can 227 
enhance the degradation of plastic through beneficial electrostatic interactions, offering new opportunities for 228 
improving bio-recycling through synergistic enzymatic activity. 229 
Conclusions 230 
Prior to this study the most significant changes in the activity of PET degrading enzymes were realised by 231 
increasing the performance of the biocatalysts at elevated temperatures, where the polymer is more dynamic. 232 
This focus on thermostability has obfuscated how other physical-chemical properties of the polymer surface 233 
influence the activity of PETases, limiting our understanding on the enzyme-substrate interactions at play. 234 
Here, by rational redesign of the surface of a highly active PETase, SfCut, we were able to investigate the impact 235 
of electrostatics on enzymatic activity in the absence of confounding factors like thermodynamic stability or 236 
changes in features near the active site13,30,32. Through carefully crafted studies on the effects of ionic strength 237 
on product released from different substrates, we demonstrate that substrate selectivity is driven by polymer 238 
morphologies having distinct electrostatic profiles, and therefore influencing enzyme-surface interactions in 239 
contrasting manners. Measured zeta potentials are known to have smaller magnitudes on rough surfaces than on 240 
smooth surfaces33,34, and the impacts of this are likely manifesting here. Within the diffuse layer around the 241 
neatively charged polymer surface, the anionic SfCut experiences repulsion while the cationic SfInv experiences 242 
attraction, with these interactions being more pronounced on smooth films than on rough powders. 243 
By focusing on electrostatic interactions governing the activity of PET degrading enzymes at the polymer surface, 244 
we demonstrated that it is possible to not only tune the selectivity of PETases for substrates of differing 245 
morphologies, but also increase their binding affinity through the rational engineering of surface charge away 246 
from the active site. The process of introducing large scale changes in protein surface electrostatic potential in 247 
SfInv also afforded substantially improved activities on all PET substrates tested, exposing the benefits of using 248 
an electrostatically-oriented approach to engineering plastic depolymerases for improved activities at the low salt 249 
concentrations relevant for industrial processes. As we demonstrate through the preservation of the 250 
thermodynamic stability of SfInv, this approach is complementary to established methods designed to enhance 251 
the thermostability of enzymes and can be introduced in the engineering process towards better industrial PET 252 
depolymerases. 253 
We also found that synergistic activity between PETases can be realised through the applications of 254 
complementarily charged proteins. While the enhancement in activity we observed on films is relatively modest, 255 
just over two-fold, this advance is the first method for building synergy into enzymes acting at plastic surfaces 256 
and opens the door to engineering campaigns specifically tailored towards improving it. In contrast to synergies 257 
between enzyme pairs acting at different chemical sites on a substrate, the mechanism of synergy in this case 258 
appears to depend upon the electrostatic masking effect of the positively charged SfInv when bound to PET, 259 
which in turn promotes the activity of the negatively charged SfCut. This mechanism is likely generalisable and 260 
opens the possibility of exploiting this discovery to reduce the enzyme loading needed for industrial processes or 261 
to create dynamic mixtures of enzymes capable of accommodating variations in surface electrostatics across 262 
substrates or over the course of depolymerisations.  263
